Stock market basic terms

Term Meaning
Candlestick Each bar shows open, high, low, and close for one session. The body is open-to-close; thin lines (wicks) are the range.
Bullish / bearish candle Bullish means the close finished above the open (often colored green). Bearish means the close finished below the open (often red).
OHLC Open, High, Low, Close — the four prices that define a single period on a candlestick chart.
Volume How many shares traded in a period. Spikes can confirm interest around breakouts or reversals.
Moving average (MA) A smoothed line of past closes. Traders often compare short vs. long MAs to judge trend.
Support / resistance Price zones where buying (support) or selling (resistance) has repeatedly appeared. Not guarantees—context matters.
Volatility How much prices swing. Higher volatility means wider candles and more uncertainty.
Bull market / bear market A bull market is a broad period of rising prices and optimism; a bear market is a sustained drop (often 20%+ from highs) and more cautious sentiment.
Market capitalisation (market cap) Total value of a company’s listed shares: share price × number of shares. Used to compare size—large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap.
P/E ratio (price-to-earnings) Share price divided by earnings per share (EPS). A rough valuation yardstick—compare within the same sector, not in isolation.
EPS (earnings per share) Company profit allocated to each share. Trends in EPS matter as much as a single number.
Dividend & dividend yield A dividend is cash paid to shareholders from profits. Yield is annual dividend per share divided by price—higher isn’t always “better” if payouts aren’t sustainable.
IPO (initial public offering) When a private company first sells shares to the public. New listings can be volatile; read the prospectus and risks.
Liquidity How easily you can buy or sell without moving the price much. Large, heavily traded names usually have better liquidity.
Bid–ask spread The gap between the best buy (bid) and best sell (ask) quote. Tighter spreads usually mean lower trading friction.
Long vs. short Long means you benefit if the price rises. Short (where allowed) means you benefit if the price falls—risk can be unlimited, so it needs strict discipline.
Stop-loss / limit order A stop-loss aims to cap loss by exiting near a set price. A limit order only fills at your price or better. Both depend on liquidity and gaps.
Index (e.g. Nifty, Sensex) A basket of stocks used to track a market or theme. You can’t buy the index directly—you trade index funds, ETFs, or derivatives that track it.
ETF (exchange-traded fund) A fund that holds many securities and trades like a stock on the exchange. Offers diversification in one ticker; check expense ratio and tracking error.
Portfolio & diversification Your combined holdings. Diversification spreads risk across assets or sectors so one bad outcome doesn’t dominate.
Blue chip Widely recognised, often large, established companies with a long track record—not risk-free, but often seen as relatively stable.
Correction A shorter, sharp pullback (often around 10% from recent highs) within an ongoing trend—different from a multi-month bear market.
